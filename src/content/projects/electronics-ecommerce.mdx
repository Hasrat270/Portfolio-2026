## The situation

The client had been selling electronics through online marketplaces for years. Sales were steady, but every transaction was costing them roughly fifteen percent in platform fees. They wanted to move to their own store and own the customer relationship — including the email list they'd never been able to build.

The catch: it had to launch in under two months, in time for the holiday season.

## What I decided

A few choices shaped the project:

- **Build it on Next.js.** Product pages had to rank well on Google — electronics shoppers search for very specific things — so server-rendering made sense.
- **Use a real database (PostgreSQL).** Products, customers, orders, inventory — these all relate to each other in messy ways. A traditional database handles that cleanly.
- **A custom admin panel, not an off-the-shelf one.** The client had a quirky workflow for B2B pricing tiers that no standard tool supported well. Better to build something that fits their day-to-day than force them to adapt.
- **Stripe for payments.** Not glamorous, but it's the right tool. Customers already trust it.

## What I traded off

I skipped a dedicated search engine for the first version and used the database's built-in search instead. It's slower under heavy load, but it kept the system simpler and cheaper to run. There's a clear path to upgrade if they grow past it.

The shopping cart lives in the browser, with a quick sync to the server at checkout. Simpler to build, but it means a customer who switches devices loses their cart. We agreed that was an acceptable trade for v1.

## How it turned out

The store went live a week ahead of schedule. The client cut their per-sale fees by roughly twelve percent on day one. Within two months they had their first proper email list and were running their first direct campaign. Sales recovered to within eight percent of their marketplace baseline despite the move — a number we were both happy with.
